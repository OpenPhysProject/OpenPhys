Goals:

  • Present the characteristics of EM radiation (wavelength, frequency, visibility, name, real-world characteristics, location, measuring units, uses….) at various spectral regions
  • Show how wave/particle effects are distributed across the electromagnetic spectrum
  • Get a sense of the scales and energies involved

Assumed Prior Learning:

Prior to interacting with the object, the learner should be able to:

  • Define and distinguish the terms “logarithmic relationship” and “linear relationship”
  • Give some examples of “frequency” and “wavelength”
  • Define “spectrum” and “electromagnetic spectrum”
  • Define “wave,” “wavelength,” “ray,” “particle” and “frequency” as photons
  • Recognize: Wave Speed = (Frequency x Wavelength)
  • Recognize: Photon Energy = (Planck’s Constant x Frequency)
  • Recognize that ‘Frequency’ is common to both wave and particle perspectives.

Learning Objectives:

As a result of manipulating the object, the learner will be able to:

  • Learn specific facts about each range on the spectrum
  • Explain how wave-particle duality is manifested in the EM spectrum:
    • Explain how wave effects generally diminish with increasing frequency
    • Explain how photon (particle) effects generally increase with increasing frequency
    • Identify specific regions of the spectrum with wave effects, particle effects and both
  • Identify how language usage changes in different spectral regions (wavelength, keV, MeV etc.)
  • State that the speed of EM waves is 3 x 10^8 m/s
  • Match characteristics of radiation with spectral region
  • Calculate conversions between Frequency, Wavelength and Photon Energy using appropriate units.

Radio waves are a type of electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths in the electromagnetic spectrum longer than infrared light. Radio waves have frequencies from 300 GHz to as low as 3 kHz, and corresponding wavelengths ranging from 1 millimeter (0.039 in) to 100 kilometers (62 mi).

Wave-like

All waves are known to undergo reflection or the bouncing off of an obstacle. Most people are very accustomed to the fact that light waves also undergo reflection. The reflection of light waves off of a mirrored surface results in the formation of an image. One characteristic of wave reflection is that the angle at which the wave approaches a flat reflecting surface is equal to the angle at which the wave leaves the surface. This characteristic is observed for water waves and sound waves.

frequency

Frequency

Frequency is the number of occurrences of a repeating event per unit time. It is also referred to as temporal frequency, which emphasizes the contrast to spatial frequency and angular frequency. The period is the duration of time of one cycle in a repeating event, so the period is the reciprocal of the frequency. For example, if a newborn baby's heart beats at a frequency of 120 times a minute, its period – the interval between beats – is half a second (60 seconds (i.e., a minute) divided by 120 beats). Frequency is an important parameter used in science and engineering to specify the rate of oscillatory and vibratory phenomena, such as mechanical vibrations, audio (sound) signals, radio waves, and light.
